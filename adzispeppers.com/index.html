Story of the Polish BBQ Pepper

The Gdańsk merchant and the "Pepper King" of the East
In the late Middle Ages, Polish cuisine was famously spicy due to robust trade links with the Orient, which made spices like black pepper much cheaper in Poland than in Western Europe. The story of this spice trade would have centered on Northern Polish port cities like Gdańsk, whose merchants would have played a key role.

Imagine a tale of a clever Gdańsk merchant named Jan, who had a fierce rival in a Western European spice trader. Their competition peaked during a lavish royal feast in Kraków. Jan knew his rival would try to impress the king with rare and exotic flavors. Instead of a new spice, Jan arrived with a massive shipment of black peppercorns from his trade routes, proclaiming, "I bring not a spice of scarcity, but the spice of a king's strength!" The price of pepper in Poland was already low, but Jan gave the king a price so favorable it would cement a long-term trading relationship. The king, known for his love of spicy food, declared Jan the "Pepper King of the East," favoring the common but potent spice over his rival's expensive exotica. This cemented Gdańsk's role as a trading powerhouse and proved that sometimes, cleverness and trade relationships are more powerful than a high-cost luxury item.
